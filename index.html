SOFTENING PARAMETER
Simulations 2 and 3 explored the impact of manipulating the softening length ("eps") and time step on the stability and accuracy of the particle system. Keeping the time step constant and varying "eps", it was found that reducing "eps" to 0.0001 resulted in a smoother and more accurate simulation. Set- ting "eps" to 1 demonstrated the circular dispersion of particles, emphasizing the need for a balanced softening length (figure 5 and figure 6). The simulations highlight the art of choosing the right "eps" value for accurate and realistic outcomes, as "eps" acts as a safety measure in calculations to avoid nu- merical problems when particles are close. The consequences of setting "eps" too high were illustrated, showing significant changes in the system with particles exhibiting unrealistic flows and fluctuations in circular velocity, cumulative mass, and surface density. In summary, these simulations emphasized the importance of selecting the softening parameter to achieve realistic and stable simulations. The results provided valuable insights into the dynamics of the simulated galaxy system under different softening lengths.
TIME STEP
In Simulations 4 and 5, the focus shifted to keeping the softening length constant while varying only the timestep. Simulation 4 explored changes in accuracy by adjusting the timestep from 0.01 (original time) to 0.1 and 0.001 for simulation 5. The accuracy decreases as the timestep increases. Smaller timesteps can enhance accuracy but may slow the simulation and use more resources. The accuracy increased when set to 0.001, revealing that smaller timesteps can resolve the system with good accuracy. However, there were minor variations in the bulge component’s surface density compared to Simulation 4, indicating that changing the timestep influences the distribution of galactic component profiles over the radius which is considerably tiny. Adjusting the timestep affects how often particle positions and velocities are updated (figure 8 and figure 9).
TIME STEP
Figure 10 depicts the evolving interaction of two galaxies with a softening length ("eps") of 0.1 and a timestep ("dtinit") of 0.01. Starting at a 30-degree tilt, the gravitational forces between the galaxies create swirling tidal tails. As they get closer, the galaxies lose their distinct disk shapes and merge into a more spherical form. The collisions result in distorted disks and eventually lead to the merging of galactic nuclei, forming a spheroidal galaxy. The last plot shows the system relaxing, with some particles still interacting. The simulation generated 400 dumps.
